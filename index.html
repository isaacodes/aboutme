Design Principles
When I design a website, I tend to stick to a very minimalistic theme. If too much is going on on a webpage, a user can feel lost, and I know that that feeling is not a pleasant one. To lessen this effect on my users, I like to make my websites easy to navigate while still maintaining a pleasing look and feel.

How I started programming

When I was 9, I had a very old and crappy computer. I didn't have any fancy text editor, and the one that I used didn't even support links. I was trying to replicate a webpage just using this text editor, so I went to my dad to ask how to add a link to the document. Instead of showing me how to do this (probably because he couldn't), my dad showed me how to make a link in HTML. Immediately, I started playing around with this. One of the first projects that I remember creating was a word scramble game, using a combination of a little CSS, HTML links, and a whole lot of web pages. For every link that someone clicked on the document, that person would be taken to a page that represented the choice they made: if it was wrong, they went back to the beginning of the word (there were 3 words). If the choice was correct, they were taken to a page that moved the correct choice to the front of the word (where it belonged), and then it went on and on and on until you got all three words. After I showed this to my dad, he told me that I should start learning JavaScript. So I did. We went to my local Barnes and Noble and picked out a book called "JavaScript for Kids" by Nick Morgan. I would ask my dad what some things were, but otherwise, I learned JavaScript from this book and Stack Overflow. Since that time, I have made a lot of projects, many of which I have since deleted, but I am also currently working on a few projects that won't be going away any time soon.
